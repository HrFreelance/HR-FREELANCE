## What Most Engagement Programs Get Wrong

The standard playbook for employee engagement in Indian SMEs goes like this: annual survey → low scores → one-off event → back to normal. The scores don't improve. Turnover doesn't change. HR gets blamed.

The problem isn't the survey. It's treating engagement as an event rather than an operating discipline.

After managing the full HR function at Kankatala Textiles — including a proactive grievance resolution program, structured one-on-ones, and culture-building initiatives — here are the five things that actually moved the needle.

---

## 1. Make Grievance Handling Genuinely Safe

The most underrated engagement lever. Employees who believe their complaints will be taken seriously — and won't result in retaliation — stay longer and perform better.

This means:
- A documented, time-bound grievance resolution process (not just "HR's door is open")
- Acknowledgement within 24 hours of a complaint, even if resolution takes longer
- Follow-up with the employee *after* resolution to confirm they feel heard

The 24-hour acknowledgement rule alone changed the culture perception at my current organisation. People stop assuming HR is management's enforcer when they experience the opposite.

---

## 2. One-on-Ones That Aren't Performance Reviews

Monthly one-on-ones between managers and direct reports, with a clear structure:

1. What's going well? (5 minutes)
2. What's getting in your way? (10 minutes)
3. What support do you need from me? (5 minutes)
4. Career and growth — anything to flag? (5 minutes)

No performance rating. No documentation going into any formal record. The goal is intelligence-gathering on emerging disengagement — catching the "I've mentally checked out" signals before they become resignation letters.

I introduced this cadence for team leads at Kankatala and within two months had early warning on two situations that would otherwise have become exits.

---

## 3. Recognition That Doesn't Feel Like a Chore

Most recognition programs in Indian companies are either too formal (annual award ceremonies that feel performative) or too random (ad-hoc "good job" emails that mean nothing).

What works:
- **Specific, immediate, public** recognition. "Rajesh caught a compliance gap last week that saved us a penalty" is worth more than a quarterly award.
- Peer-to-peer nomination channels (a simple WhatsApp group or a Slack channel works fine)
- Connecting the recognition to the company value it exemplifies

It costs nothing. The consistent application of it costs attention — which is why most managers don't do it.

---

## 4. Career Visibility (Not Just Career Development)

There's a difference between "we have a training budget" and "Priya knows what her path looks like here in 18 months." Most disengagement I've observed comes from the second problem, not the first.

A practical approach:
- Semi-annual conversations (separate from performance reviews) focused exclusively on career trajectory
- Make internal mobility visible — when a role opens internally, tell existing employees before posting externally
- Document skill development goals in the HRMS and actually follow up on them

One of the most common exit interview themes I've collected is: *"I didn't feel like I was going anywhere."* Career visibility is the antidote.

---

## 5. The Exit Interview as a Learning Tool (Not a Formality)

Most exit interviews in SMEs are either a box-ticking exercise or an attempt to retain someone who has already decided to leave. Neither is useful.

The goal of an exit interview is *institutional learning*. Questions that generate actionable data:

- "When did you first start thinking about leaving?"
- "What would have made you stay?"
- "What would you tell your replacement about what to watch out for?"
- "What's one thing we could fix that would help the next person in your role?"

I document every exit interview and present a quarterly theme analysis to leadership. If the same answer appears three times, it's a pattern — not bad luck.

---

## The Common Thread

None of these five practices require budget. They require *consistency* and *follow-through*. HR teams that implement them intermittently get intermittent results. The organisations that make these operating disciplines — recurring, documented, reviewed — see attrition drop and engagement scores follow.
